There is SO much more to mechanical keyboards than your standard Razer gamer keyboard that glows like a rainbow and clicks away like you're shaking a jar of LEGOs. The customizability of mechanical keyboards is what drew me in. I was skeptical at first. I thought my standard laptop keyboard was fine, got the job done. Little did I know that I was ignoring a whole new typing experience. Mechanical keyboards are some of the smoothest keys I've typed on, almost like you are typing on clouds. And they don't always come in that clicky variety. There are silent switches that sound so muffled you can barely hear that you are typing. I love the typing experience but I also love the creative space mechanical keyboards provide. If you ever wished your keyboard had just that shade of green with just that specific shade of orange, you can make it happen or it may even have been done before. Building keyboards is relatively simple as well, but it has that relaxing effect of doing repetitive tasks (soldering the switches, putting on the keycaps over and over). Ultimately, it was just trying something new and loving it that drew me in to this hobby.
While many might not term this a "hobby" per se, I believe one of the things I love to do is try new things! I am always open to new experiences, and many times saying yes to something new that may be intimidating has turned out amazing! Some examples include deciding to study abroad in Iceland for a summer, going to various cultural and food fairs with friends, travelling to Oregon to watch the eclipse, and (seen above) attending a lights festival.

My Work

This is a brief overview of some key projects that I have undertaken in my undegraduate career. Please click on the visuals to open up a description that illustrates my projects in greater detail. For more details about my technical skills and previous coursework, please view my resume above.

At my internship with IDEX Health and Science, I conducted a Design of Experiement (DOE) model. The primary goal was to examine and find trends within torque data retrieved from valves varying in pressure rating and rotor seal material. In total, 30 valves were tested with 5 in each independent group. Trends from the data recorded were presented to key engineers in the Analytical Instruments and Advanced Systems Development teams. The analytical data can be used to facillitate future valve designs for various consumer needs.
After following a series of ANSI working drawings, I created all the individual components of a gyroscope in SolidWorks and assembled it. Using this understanding of the gyroscrope, I utilized my campus's machine shop to fabricate each component using the bandsaw, drill press, lathe, mill, and CNC machine. Afterward, I assembled the parts and test it as shown above.
Another project I undertook during my internship with IDEX Health and Science was the design and prototyping of a fixture for a pump. After taking measurements of the machine it was to be embedded in and discussing several designs with the engineers, I created an initial design to be shown to the head engineer. I then prototyped the design using aluminum sheet metal in the company's machine shop. Through manufacturing the design, I became aware of potential pitfalls in mass production of this design. To streamline the process, I revised my design to be symmetrical for easier fabrication. The design was later approved and officially ordered for assembly in the final machine.
I participated in the Biomedical Engineering Society's annual Make-a-Thon competition. The prompt consisted of designing a device to aid users with physical and/or visual impairments accurately and comfortably dispense eye drops. The design my team finalized was an automated system in which a spring-loaded one-stop eyedropper holder has a microcontrolled piston that compresses the eyedropper to dispense precisely one drop at a time. An ergonomic design coupled with a grip allows for increased dexterity for consumers, and an anti-blinking system eliminates the problem in which users frequently blink when anticipating the drop to fall. My role in this project was the CAD designer. I collaborated with a peer of mine in the design of the individual components and final assembly of the device.
In my Embedded Systems Design course, I was given a TI CC3200 LaunchPad to proram using Code Composer Studio and learn more about the communication of electronic devices and the modules used in real-time embedded systems. This included labs that utilized Universal Asynchronous Receiver/Trasmitters (UART), Serial Peripheral Interfaces, Inter-integrated circuits, ADCs, timers, and Interrupt Handlers. Key projects was include creating a simple audio interface in which the embedded system would decode dual-tone multi-frequency (DTMF) audio signals and use the input to compose messages that would print on an OLED screen which could be sent to a connected LaunchPad through a UART communication channel. Another project was utilizing RESTful API to securely connect to Amazon Web Services (AWS) and send and retrieve data from the AWS cloud. This was linked the AWS IoT Service so that a message composed on the board would be sent to the cloud and then sent through SMS to a given phone number.
